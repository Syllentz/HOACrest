Governing Documents

Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (DCCR) View Full Document

What it is: This is the master legal document for Mason Woods — think of it as the community’s “constitution.” It establishes all the rules that run with the land, meaning they apply to every current and future homeowner. It covers property rights, assessments (HOA dues), architectural controls, common area rules, and the Association’s enforcement powers. Recorded under Clerk’s File No. RP-2022-378389 in Harris County.

Key Provisions
  • Defines what you can and cannot do with your property, from exterior modifications to land useSee p.1 →
  • Establishes mandatory annual assessments (HOA dues) that fund community maintenance
  • Creates the Architectural Review Committee (ARC) that must approve exterior changes
  • Gives the Association authority to enforce rules, levy fines, and place liens on property
  • Defines common areas, easements, and the Association’s maintenance responsibilities
Bylaws View Full Document

What it is: The Bylaws are the Association’s internal operating manual. They explain how the Board of Directors is elected, how meetings are run, and how decisions get made. If the DCCR is the “what,” the Bylaws are the “how.”

Key Provisions
  • Board of Directors structure — how many members, how they’re elected, term lengths
  • Meeting procedures — annual meetings, special meetings, notice requirements
  • Voting rules — quorum requirements, proxy voting, ballot procedures
  • Officer roles and duties — President, Vice President, Secretary, Treasurer
  • Committee authority — how committees like the ARC are created and empowered
Supplemental Amendments (Sections 1, 4, 7 & 8)

What they are: These four amendments officially bring Sections 1, 4, 7, and 8 of the neighborhood into Mason Woods. Each one adds section-specific rules and important environmental notices about the land — things like detention areas, restricted reserves, gas easements, and wildlife habitats near your property.

Key Provisions (same across all sections)
  • All homes must have at least 1,200 square feet of living areaSec 1 p.3 →
  • Environmental condition notices — restricted reserves, detention ponds, and sensitive areas near your lotSec 1 p.4 →
  • Utility and gas transmission easements — you cannot build on these areas
  • Homeowners assume liability for environmental conditions and must not contaminate restricted areas
  • Full indemnification of the Association and Declarant from environmental claims

Property & Architectural Standards

Architectural Modification Guidelines View Full Document

What it means for you: Planning to build a patio cover, put up a fence, paint your house, or add a pool? You need to read this first. Every exterior modification to your home requires approval from the Architectural Review Committee (ARC) before you start work. This document spells out exactly what’s allowed for 24 types of projects — materials, dimensions, colors, and locations.

Key Rules by Project Type
  • Arbors/Pergolas: Max 12 ft height, 300 sq ft. Decay-resistant materials. No enclosure walls allowed.See p.3 →
  • Basketball Goals: 1 per lot. Portable goals max 10 ft from facade. Metal pole, plexiglass/fiberglass backboard. No chain nets.See p.3 →
  • Building Additions: Must match existing home style. Cannot exceed 1/3 of remaining backyard. Engineered drawings required.See p.5 →
  • Decks/Patios: Backyard only. Max 18″ height, 300 sq ft. Must be 5 ft from rear and 3 ft from side property lines.See p.5 →
  • Driveways: Max 18 ft wide. Concrete and rebar only. Extensions cannot be just for extra parking.See p.6 →
  • Exterior Paint: Earth tones only. Must be harmonious with brick and roof. Trim may be deeper shade.See p.6 →
  • Fences: Cedar pickets, pine posts. No chain link, chicken wire, or lattice. Stain only — no paint. Dog-eared style.See p.7 →
  • Landscaping: Live plants only — no artificial. Black or brown mulch only (no red). Cannot obstruct sight lines.See p.8 →
  • Patio Covers: Wood or aluminum only. Must integrate with existing roofline. Shingles must match home. 7–12 ft height.See p.9 →
  • Pools/Spas: No above-ground pools. Rear yard only. 3 ft from property lines. $1,000 deposit if accessing through Association landscape.See p.11 →
  • Storage Buildings: Max 9 ft tall, 120 sq ft. Must match home materials/colors. On 4″ concrete slab. No corrugated roofs.See p.13 →
  • Front Yard Accessories: Max 4 items, max 18″ tall. No bird baths, free-standing flagpoles, or park benches.See p.14 →
  • Perimeter Fencing: Steel only, 5–6 ft, powder coated black. Min 4″ between vertical bars. 3 ft from sidewalks.See p.15 →
Solar Energy Devices and Roofing Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: Thinking about solar panels or a new roof? Solar devices are allowed but must meet specific location and aesthetic requirements. Roofing materials must be wind-resistant and match the look of the neighborhood. ARC approval is required before installation.

Key Provisions
  • Solar panels are permitted on rooftops or within fenced yards that meet criteria
  • Panels should be as inconspicuous as possible and compatible with roof aesthetics
  • Approved roofing: wind-resistant, energy-efficient materials matching subdivision look
  • ARC approval required for all solar installations and roofing material changes
Swimming Pool Enclosures Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: If you have a pool or spa, you need a proper safety enclosure around it. This policy tells you exactly what type of fencing is acceptable. Good news: standard black mesh pool fences are pre-approved — you don’t even need to apply to the ARC.

Key Provisions
  • Enclosure fence must be non-climbable and max 6 feet tall
  • Pre-approved (no ARC needed): Black transparent mesh enclosures under 6 ft
  • Other designs/materials require ARC approval before installation
  • Must meet all state and local pool safety requirements
Security Measures Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: Want to add security fencing to your lot? This policy covers what types of security fences are approved under Texas law. The Association isn’t responsible for your security — you assume all risks and costs.

Key Provisions
  • Approved style: 6-foot black steel flat-top metal fencing with specific specifications
  • All security fence installations require ARC approval before work begins
  • Owners assume all risks and must indemnify the Association from any security-related claims

Homeowner Policies

Flag Display Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: You can fly the U.S. flag, Texas flag, or a military branch flag at your home. There are rules on flagpole height and placement, but a simple wall-mounted pole on your house doesn’t even need ARC approval if it follows the guidelines.

Key Provisions
  • One flagpole per lotSee p.1 →
  • Allowed flags: U.S., Texas, or U.S. military branch flags onlySee p.2 →
  • Freestanding poles: max 20 ft tall, backyard preferred, ARC approval requiredSee p.2 →
  • Wall-mounted poles: max 5 ft long, front or rear of house, no ARC needed if compliantSee p.2 →
  • Replace faded/torn flags immediately; halyards must not make noiseSee p.3 →
  • Evening lighting allowed: max 2 bulbs, 150W total, no light spill onto neighborsSee p.3 →
Display of Religious Items Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: You’re allowed to display religious items on your property as long as they reflect your sincere religious beliefs. Small items (under 25 square inches) can go up without any paperwork. Larger displays need an ARC application.

Key Provisions
  • Religious displays are generally permitted on your lot or homeSee p.2 →
  • Items 25 square inches or smaller — no ARC approval neededSee p.2 →
  • Larger displays require ARC application with description and site planSee p.2 →
  • Not allowed on common/Association property, traffic signs, or fire hydrantsSee p.2 →
Rain Barrel Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: Rain barrels are allowed under Texas law, but there are restrictions on where you can place them and how they look. They generally can’t be visible from the street, and you must take steps to prevent mosquito breeding. ARC approval is required.

Key Provisions
  • Not allowed on Association or common property, or between your home and the street
  • If visible, must meet professional-grade standards and be properly screened
  • Mosquito prevention measures are mandatory
  • Colors must comply with community standards; ARC approval required
Standby Electric Generator Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: You can install a permanent standby generator (like a Generac) for backup power, but there are rules. It must be professionally installed, enclosed in a sound-attenuating case, and screened from view. You’ll need ARC approval before installation.

Key Provisions
  • Minimum 7 kW capacity; must be in a manufacturer’s sound enclosureSee p.2 →
  • Must be installed by licensed contractors (electrical, plumbing, fuel lines)See p.2 →
  • Must be screened if visible from the street or neighborsSee p.3 →
  • Testing only allowed between 10:00 AM and 4:00 PMSee p.3 →
  • Preferred location: side or back of home, outside easements and setback linesSee p.3 →
  • Only for emergency backup — cannot replace utility power for regular useSee p.4 →

Administrative & Financial

Collection, Board Hearing and Payment Plan Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: This explains what happens if you fall behind on your HOA dues. The Association follows a step-by-step process before taking serious action. You have rights along the way — including the right to a hearing and the option to set up a payment plan.

Key Provisions
  • Assessments are due annually; become delinquent 30 days after due dateSee p.2 →
  • Interest accrues on unpaid balances after 30 daysSee p.2 →
  • Process: Past Due Notice → Final Notice (certified mail) → collections/attorneySee p.3 →
  • You get at least 45 days to pay before further actionSee p.3 →
  • You can request a Board hearing within 30 days of the Final NoticeSee p.4 →
  • Payment plans available: minimum 3 months, equal monthly paymentsSee p.7 →
  • Common area access may be suspended if no hearing requested within 30 daysSee p.4 →
  • Foreclosure is possible as a last resort under Texas Property CodeSee p.6 →
Enforcement, Board Hearing and Fine Policy View Full Document

What it means for you: If you violate a community rule, this is the process that follows. The Association doesn’t jump straight to fines — they start with a friendly letter and give you time to fix the issue. Fines only kick in if you don’t respond. You always have the right to request a hearing.

Key Provisions
  • Curable violations (things you can fix): parking, maintenance, barking dogs, unauthorized modificationsSee p.2 →
  • Uncurable violations (one-time events): fireworks, property damage, unauthorized garage salesSee p.4 →
  • Step 1: Courtesy Letter (informal — “please fix this”)See p.3 →
  • Step 2: Violation Letter (formal notice with deadline)See p.3 →
  • Step 3: Demand Letter with fine amount, right to cure, and hearing optionSee p.3 →
  • Fine for curable violations: up to $100 per day the violation continuesSee p.7 →
  • You can request a Board hearing within 30 days of the Demand LetterSee p.5 →
  • Repeat violations within 6 months: fines start immediately without a new Demand LetterSee p.5 →
